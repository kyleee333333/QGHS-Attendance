Chemical Reactions — Redox

5-slide overview · definitions, balancing, examples, applications

Slide 1 — What is a Redox Reaction?

A redox reaction (reduction–oxidation reaction) is a chemical reaction in which electrons are transferred between species. One reactant loses electrons (is oxidized) and another gains electrons (is reduced).

Key ideas

  • Oxidation = loss of electrons → increase in oxidation state.
  • Reduction = gain of electrons → decrease in oxidation state.
  • Redox always involves both processes simultaneously: when one species is oxidized, another is reduced.
  • Many important processes are redox: corrosion, metabolism, combustion, electrochemistry.

Symbols & notation

Electron transfer shown as e- in half-reactions. Oxidation numbers (states) help track electron movement.

Generic form (overall):
A + B → Aⁿ⁺ + Bᵐ⁻ (illustrative — shows one species oxidized, the other reduced)
Tip: If a species loses electrons, add electrons on the product side of its half-reaction; if it gains electrons, add electrons to the reactant side.

Slide 2 — Oxidation Numbers & How to Identify Redox

Oxidation number (state) is a formal charge assignment used to track electrons in compounds. Rules (brief):

  • Element in elemental form: oxidation state = 0 (e.g., O2, Fe).
  • Monatomic ion: equals its charge (Na+ = +1, Cl- = -1).
  • Oxygen usually −2 (except in peroxides where it is −1); hydrogen is usually +1 (except in metal hydrides).
  • Sum of oxidation numbers in a neutral compound = 0; in a polyatomic ion = ion charge.

Identify redox

Compare oxidation numbers of each element in reactants and products. If any element's oxidation number changes, the reaction is a redox reaction.

Example: 2 Na + Cl2 → 2 NaCl
Na: 0 → +1 (oxidized) ; Cl: 0 → −1 (reduced)
Presenter notes: Walk students through the rules with examples. Emphasize the usefulness for complex molecules and ionic reactions. Show a non-redox example, e.g., double displacement without electron-transfer, to contrast.

Slide 3 — Half-Reactions & Balancing Redox Equations

A convenient method for balancing redox equations is the half-reaction method. Steps (acidic solutions):

  1. Write oxidation and reduction half-reactions separately.
  2. Balance atoms other than H and O.
  3. Balance O by adding H2O and H by adding H+.
  4. Balance charge by adding electrons (e-).
  5. Multiply half-reactions so electron counts match, then add and cancel electrons.

Example (acidic): Permanganate + Iron(II)

Unbalanced: MnO4- + Fe2+ → Mn2+ + Fe3+
Half-reactions:
Reduction: MnO4- + 8 H+ + 5 e- → Mn2+ + 4 H2O
Oxidation: Fe2+ → Fe3+ + e-
Multiply oxidation half by 5 to match electrons:
5 Fe2+ → 5 Fe3+ + 5 e-
Add them and cancel electrons:
MnO4- + 8 H+ + 5 Fe2+ → Mn2+ + 4 H2O + 5 Fe3+
For basic solutions, add OH- to neutralize H+ (convert to H2O), then simplify.
Presenter notes: Work this example step-by-step with students. Explain why we add H+/H2O and how electron cancellation ensures charge conservation.

Slide 4 — Simple Worked Examples

Example A — Zinc and Copper(II)

Reaction: Zn + Cu2+ → Zn2+ + Cu

Oxidation half: Zn → Zn2+ + 2 e-
Reduction half: Cu2+ + 2 e- → Cu
Combined: Zn + Cu2+ → Zn2+ + Cu

This is the basis of a simple galvanic cell: Zn oxidizes (anode), Cu2+ reduces (cathode).

Example B — Balancing by Inspection (quick method)

Some redox equations can be balanced by inspection when electron transfer is obvious. Always check mass and charge balance after finishing.

Practice question
Balance the following in acidic solution:
Cr2O72- + Fe2+ → Cr3+ + Fe3+
Balanced: Cr2O72- + 14 H+ + 6 Fe2+ → 2 Cr3+ + 6 Fe3+ + 7 H2O
Presenter notes: Emphasize common electron numbers for transition metal changes (e.g., Fe2+ → Fe3+ is 1 e-, MnO4- → Mn2+ is 5 e-). Encourage students to practice half-reaction pairing.

Slide 5 — Applications, Summary & Quick Quiz

Real-world applications

  • Batteries: Galvanic cells generate electricity by spontaneous redox (e.g., alkaline, lead–acid, lithium batteries).
  • Corrosion: Rusting of iron is a redox process (Fe → Fe2+/Fe3+), often prevented by sacrificial anodes.
  • Industrial redox: Processes like metal extraction and electroplating rely on controlled redox reactions.
  • Biological redox: Cellular respiration and photosynthesis are sequences of redox steps that transfer electrons to produce ATP or reduce CO2.

Quick summary

  • Redox = electron transfer (oxidation = loss, reduction = gain).
  • Oxidation numbers help identify changes and track electrons.
  • Half-reaction method balances atoms, charge, and electrons — works in acidic and basic media.
  • Redox principles are central across chemistry, biology, engineering, and environmental science.

Mini quiz (self-check)

  1. In the reaction Cu + 2 Ag+ → Cu2+ + 2 Ag, which species is oxidized?
  2. Balance: S + HNO3 → SO2 + NO (acidic) — identify main steps.
  3. Why does adding an external circuit allow a galvanic cell to produce current?
Answers (brief): 1) Cu is oxidized (Cu → Cu2+); 3) The external circuit allows electrons to flow from anode to cathode, creating electrical current while ions move internally to maintain charge balance.
Presenter notes: Finish by tying redox back to previous topics (stoichiometry, acids/bases for aqueous balancing) and suggest practice: balance a mix of transition-metal redox reactions in acidic/basic media.
